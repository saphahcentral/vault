TITLE: The Builders of Stone
CODEX: OC3
DOCUMENT: OC3-006
EPOCH: The Tartarus Age
SECTION: Megalithic Civilizations

Long before the cities of recorded history, before the rise of empires whose names fill the pages of textbooks, the earth already bore the marks of builders whose works defied ordinary explanation.

Across continents and oceans, massive stone structures appeared in places where later populations insisted they had not been the original architects. Enormous blocks of rock were cut, transported, and fitted together with astonishing precision. Walls of stone weighing many tons stood without mortar, yet their joints were so tight that even a blade could not pass between them.

These constructions are often called megaliths, a word meaning “great stone.” But the term alone does not capture the mystery surrounding their creation.

Some stones weigh dozens of tons. Others weigh hundreds. A few are estimated to exceed a thousand tons. Even with modern equipment, moving such blocks requires elaborate planning and powerful machinery.

Yet these structures appear in cultures that, according to conventional timelines, possessed only primitive tools.

The contradiction has puzzled researchers for generations.

Who were the builders of these stones?

Ancient traditions frequently give the same answer: giants.

In many parts of the world, local legends say that the first walls, the first terraces, and the first sacred platforms were built by enormous beings who possessed extraordinary strength and knowledge.

Later generations inherited these structures and built their own temples and cities upon the same foundations.

In South America, the massive stoneworks of the Andes display remarkable engineering. At sites high in the mountains, blocks of granite and andesite were cut and shaped with complex interlocking patterns. The walls curve and shift with the landscape as if they were designed to endure earthquakes and time itself.

In the Mediterranean world, ancient authors described walls so immense that they believed only the Cyclopes — legendary giants — could have built them. These “Cyclopean” walls became part of Greek mythology, but the stones themselves still exist.

Across Europe and Asia, vast stone circles, dolmens, and standing monoliths appear in fields, forests, and remote landscapes. Some are aligned with the rising and setting of the sun during solstices and equinoxes. Others point toward stars whose cycles repeat over long periods of time.

These alignments suggest that the builders possessed sophisticated astronomical knowledge.

Stone, once placed, endures.

Unlike wood or clay, stone resists the slow destruction of weather and time. When civilizations collapse, their stone foundations remain as silent witnesses to forgotten ages.

For this reason, megalithic architecture acts as a kind of memory embedded within the earth itself.

The builders who shaped these stones understood the permanence of their work.

They chose materials that would last far beyond their own lifetimes.

But the stones also raise deeper questions.

How were these blocks cut with such precision?

How were they transported across difficult terrain?

How were they lifted and positioned with such accuracy?

Some researchers have proposed lost technologies. Others suggest forgotten engineering techniques that relied on large organized labor forces.

Still others believe the answer lies in knowledge that has not yet been rediscovered.

Within the framework of the Obsidian Codex, these megalithic structures represent physical evidence that an advanced tradition existed before the earliest chapters of recorded history.

Civilizations can develop remarkable abilities through methods very different from contemporary technology.

A deep understanding of mathematics, geometry, and astronomy could produce architectural achievements that appear mysterious to later cultures.

When the Tartarus civilization collapsed, much of its knowledge vanished with it.

Libraries burned.

Cities were abandoned.

Languages disappeared.

The survivors who inherited the world preserved only fragments of what had come before.

Over generations those fragments transformed into myths and sacred traditions.

Stories of giants lifting stones.

Legends of gods teaching humans how to build.

Memories of ancient teachers who possessed wisdom greater than that of later kings.

The ruins remained, but their creators were forgotten.

Yet the stones themselves still stand.

Silent witnesses to a world that once possessed knowledge now lost to time.
