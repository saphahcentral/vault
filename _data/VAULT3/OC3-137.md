TITLE: What the Torah Says About Markings in the Skin
CODEX: OC3-137
EPOCH: Epoch V — Law and Instruction
SECTION: Section U — Body and Covenant

The Torah provides direct instruction regarding markings placed in the skin.

A specific command is given forbidding the cutting of the flesh and the engraving of marks.

This instruction establishes a boundary concerning how the body is to be treated.

The body is understood as belonging to YHVH and not solely to the individual.

Markings are associated with practices of surrounding nations and their systems.

These practices include mourning rituals and forms of idolatrous expression.

The prohibition separates the people from these external systems.

This establishes identity through obedience rather than imitation.

The instruction reflects a deeper principle concerning alignment and covenant.

What is done to the body can represent agreement with a system or authority.

The absence of markings becomes a visible distinction of obedience.

This distinction reinforces separation from foreign practices.

The command operates as both a physical and spiritual boundary.

It protects against unintended participation in opposing systems.

The instruction remains relevant as a principle beyond its original context.

The body is preserved as a space free from unauthorized inscriptions.

This maintains clarity of identity and alignment within the covenant.
